[5] 1. Introduction 
[6] Accurately tracking the interface between immiscible fluids is a fundamental challenge in multiphase computational fluid dynamics (CFD). Under large deformations, standard scalar transport schemes fail because numerical diffusion artificially smears the interface, eventually destroying the phase boundary.
[7] Phase-field methods resolve this by representing the interface as a narrow transition zone within a scalar fluid fraction field (φ). To counteract numerical smearing, a regularization term is added to the transport equation to actively maintain the interface at a prescribed thickness. Building upon the standard Conservative Diffuse Interface (CDI) method, the Accurate Conservative Diffuse Interface (ACDI) method (Jain, 2022) significantly improves the computation of the interface normal, yielding higher geometric accuracy during severe deformation without adding computational cost.
[8] This report documents the implementation and verification of four progressively refined phase-field solvers. Beginning with a first-order upwind scheme and culminating in the full ACDI method with skew-symmetric advection splitting, each numerical upgrade is benchmarked quantitatively against Jain's published data. Code performance is validated using two canonical test cases: pure advection of a translating drop, and a time-reversed shear flow that subjects the interface to extreme topological stretching and recovery.
[9] 
[10] 2. Governing Equations & Methods
[11] 2.1 Scalar Transport
	The volume fraction field φ ∈ [0,1] labels the two fluid phases — φ=1 inside the drop and φ=0 in the surrounding gas. In the absence of regularization, φ is governed by the scalar transport equation:
[12] 	∂φ/∂t + ∇·(uφ) = 0	(1)
[13] where u is the prescribed divergence-free velocity field. This equation is exact — it states that φ is simply carried by the flow with no creation or destruction. However, any finite numerical scheme introduces truncation error when solving this equation. For first-order upwind schemes, this error is structurally identical to a diffusion term:
[14] 	∂φ/∂t + u∂φ/∂x ≈ (u·Δx/2)·∂²φ/∂x²	(2)
[15] This artificial diffusion continuously smears the interface, spreading the sharp transition between φ=0 and φ=1 across more and more cells over time. The interface loses its definition and the simulation becomes physically unreliable. All three regularization-based methods in this work exist to counteract this effect.

2.2 CDI Regularization
	The Conservative Diffuse Interface method adds an explicit regularization term to the right-hand side of the transport equation:
[16] 	∂φ/∂t + ∇·(uφ) = ∇·{ Γ[ ε∇φ − φ(1−φ)·∇φ/|∇φ| ] }	(3)
[17] The right-hand side contains two competing terms. The diffusion term Γε∇φ spreads the interface outward, while the sharpening term −Γφ(1−φ)·∇φ/|∇φ| compresses it inward. These two terms reach equilibrium at a prescribed interface thickness ε, producing the steady-state hyperbolic tangent profile:
[18] 	φ = (1/2)[ 1 + tanh((R−r)/2ε) ]	(4)
[19] The factor φ(1−φ) acts as a switch — it is zero in the bulk phases (φ=0 or φ=1) and maximum at the interface (φ=0.5), ensuring the sharpening term acts only where the interface is. The rate parameter Γ controls how fast the regularization acts and must satisfy:
[20] 	Γ ≥ |u|_max / (2ε − 1)*	(5)
[21] where ε* = ε/Δx is the interface thickness in units of the grid spacing. This stability condition means that as ε decreases toward 0.5Δx, Γ must increase rapidly, making the scheme more expensive and ultimately less accurate.

2.3 ACDI Regularization
	The Accurate Conservative Diffuse Interface method proposed by Jain (2022) replaces the φ-based sharpening term with one expressed in terms of a signed-distance-like variable ψ:
[22] 	∂φ/∂t + ∇·(uφ) = ∇·{ Γ[ ε∇φ − (1/4)(1−tanh²(ψ/2ε))·∇ψ/|∇ψ| ] }	(6)
[23] where ψ is defined as:
[24] 	ψ = ε · ln( (φ+ω) / (1−φ+ω) )	(7)
[25] with ω = 10⁻¹⁰⁰ preventing division by zero. Near the interface ψ behaves nearly linearly — in contrast to φ which follows a hyperbolic tangent curve. Because ψ is smoother, its discrete gradient ∇ψ has smaller truncation error than ∇φ. This gives a more accurate estimate of the interface unit normal, reducing the grid-alignment distortion that causes CDI interfaces to become boxy under long-time deformation.
[26] The diffusion term remains expressed in φ rather than ψ. This is deliberate — near φ=0 or φ=1, ψ diverges to ±∞, and expressing diffusion in ψ would risk sign changes that destroy boundedness. Keeping diffusion in φ guarantees φ stays within [0,1] throughout the simulation.
[27] The ACDI stability condition is simply:
[28] 	Γ ≥ |u|_max	(8)
[29] This is independent of ε. Unlike CDI, reducing ε in ACDI does not require increasing Γ — the method becomes simultaneously cheaper and more accurate as the interface is made sharper.

2.4 Skew-Symmetric Advection Splitting|
	Task 4 pairs ACDI regularization with a skew-symmetric splitting of the advection operator (Jain 2022, Eq. 20):
[30] 	−∇·(uφ) → −(1/2)∇·(uφ) − (1/2)u·∇φ	(9)
[31] For a continuously divergence-free velocity field (∇·u = 0), these two forms are mathematically identical. At the discrete level they differ because the numerical velocity is not exactly divergence-free. The skew-symmetric form cancels the anti-symmetric part of the aliasing error, reducing spurious oscillations near the interface and improving energy stability. This splitting is what allows ACDI to use central differencing without the instability that affects plain central schemes.

[35] 3. Numerical Implementation
[36] 3.1 Mesh and Domain 
	All simulations are performed on a uniform two-dimensional Cartesian grid over the unit square domain [0,1]². The domain is discretized into N×N cells of equal size Δx = Δy = 1/N, with the volume fraction φ stored at cell centers. Periodic boundary conditions are applied in both spatial directions. The specific resolution used for each test case is given in Table 2.

3.2 Initial Condition
	The circular drop is initialized using the equilibrium hyperbolic tangent profile:
[37] 	φ(x,y,0) = (1/2)[ 1 + tanh((R−r)/2ε) ]	(10)
[38] where r is the minimum-image distance from the drop center, computed with periodic wrapping. This profile is the exact steady-state solution of both CDI and ACDI regularization in the absence of advection, ensuring the regularization terms are in equilibrium from the first timestep.

3.3 Velocity Fields
	Two analytically divergence-free velocity fields are prescribed. Uniform advection (Test Case 1) consists of a constant horizontal velocity u = 5i, with no vertical component. Oscillating shear flow (Test Case 2, Jain 2022 Eq. 23) is defined as:
[39] 	u = −sin²(πx) sin(2πy) cos(πt/T)	(11)
[40] 	v = sin(2πx) sin²(πy) cos(πt/T)	(12)
[41] with period T = 4. The cosine temporal factor reverses the entire velocity field at t = T/2 = 2, so an exact solution method would recover the initial drop shape at t = T = 4. Velocity components are evaluated at face positions on a staggered grid — u at x-faces and v at y-faces — as required by the conservative finite volume flux assembly.
[42] For the shear flow, the regularization rate Γ is computed from the global maximum velocity magnitude evaluated at t = 0, when the flow is at full strength. Using the instantaneous velocity would cause Γ to approach zero at t = T/2 when the flow momentarily stops, removing regularization support precisely when the interface undergoes flow reversal.
[43] 
[44] 3.4 Advective Flux Discretization
	Three advection schemes are employed across the four tasks. First-order upwind (Tasks 1 and 2) selects the face value from the upstream cell based on the sign of the normal velocity. This introduces numerical diffusion proportional to u·Δx/2 but guarantees φ remains bounded within [0,1].
[45] 	Second-order central differencing (Task 3) evaluates the face value as the arithmetic average of the two neighboring cell-center values. This scheme is non-dissipative and second-order accurate in space, but requires the CDI regularization to provide stability near sharp gradients.
[46] Skew-symmetric splitting (Task 4, Jain 2022 Eq. 20) decomposes the advection operator as:
[47] 	Adv(φ) = −(1/2)∇·(uφ) − (1/2)u·∇φ	(13)
[48] For incompressible flow these two terms are analytically equivalent, but their discrete combination cancels the anti-symmetric component of the aliasing error. This reduces spurious oscillations near the interface without introducing artificial dissipation, and is the advection scheme used in the paper's primary ACDI results. The flux divergence for all schemes is assembled in the standard finite volume form, with the net flux difference across opposing faces divided by the cell width, returning −∇·(uφ) as the advective contribution to dφ/dt.

3.5 Regularization Flux Discretization — Jain (2022) Eq. (21)
	Both CDI and ACDI regularization are implemented in conservative divergence form following Jain (2022) Eq. (21). The regularization contribution to dφ/dt is:
[49] 	R = Γ · ∇ · A	(14)
[50] where A is the regularization flux vector assembled at cell faces. Following the overbar notation of Eq. (21), face quantities are obtained as arithmetic averages of values evaluated at the two neighboring cell centers. CDI regularization flux at face (i+1/2) in the x-direction:
[51] 	A_x = ε · (∂φ/∂x)_face − φ̄(1−φ̄) · n̂_x	(15)
[52] The diffusion gradient (∂φ/∂x)_face is computed as a compact two-point difference across the face. The interface unit normal n̂ is computed at each cell center using a three-point central difference stencil in both x and y directions before normalization, then arithmetically averaged to the face:
[53] 	n̂_cc = ∇φ_cc / |∇φ_cc|, (∂φ/∂x)cc = (φ{i+1} − φ_{i−1}) / 2Δx	(16)
[54] 	n̂_face = (1/2)(n̂_cc[i] + n̂_cc[i+1])	(17)
[55] This three-point cell-center stencil for the normal is critical. Using a compact two-point face gradient for the normal direction produces noise-dominated estimates at fine grids because the gradient magnitude is poorly conditioned for a nearly flat interface resolved over only two cells. The three-point stencil spans a wider support and produces a well-conditioned normal estimate at all resolutions tested. ACDI regularization flux at face (i+1/2) in the x-direction:
[56] 	A_x = ε · (∂φ/∂x)_face − c̄ · n̂_ψ	(18)
[57] where the sharpening coefficient c and the interface normal n̂_ψ are both expressed in terms of the signed-distance-like variable ψ:
[58] 	ψ = ε · ln( (φ + ω) / (1 − φ + ω) ), ω = 10⁻¹⁰⁰	(19)
[59] 	c = (1/4)(1 − tanh²(ψ/2ε)), n̂_ψ = ∇ψ / |∇ψ|	(20)
[60] The coefficient c is evaluated after averaging ψ to the face, and the normal n̂_ψ is averaged from cell-center psi-based normals following the same procedure as CDI. The diffusion term remains expressed in φ rather than ψ to guarantee boundedness — near φ = 0 or φ = 1, ψ diverges to ±∞ and expressing diffusion in ψ would risk sign changes that violate φ ∈ [0,1].
[61] Near the interface ψ behaves nearly linearly, in contrast to φ which follows a hyperbolic tangent curve. Because the higher-order derivatives of ψ are approximately zero at the interface, the discrete gradient ∇ψ has smaller truncation error than ∇φ. This produces a more geometrically accurate interface normal, reducing the grid-alignment distortion that causes CDI interfaces to deform under long-time advection.

3.6 Time Integration
	Forward Euler (Tasks 1 and 2) is first-order accurate in time, requiring one right-hand side evaluation per timestep. Stability requires CFL = |u|Δt/Δx ≤ 1. Classical fourth-order Runge-Kutta (Tasks 3 and 4) achieves fourth-order temporal accuracy through four right-hand side evaluations per timestep at intermediate times tⁿ, tⁿ + Δt/2, and tⁿ + Δt. For the shear flow test case the velocity field is time-dependent, and each stage of the RK4 integration evaluates the velocity at the correct intermediate time, accurately capturing the continuous flow reversal.

[67] 4. Test Case 1: Drop Advection
[68] 4.1 Problem Setup
	A circular drop of radius R = 0.15 is initialized at the center of the unit domain (x₀, y₀) = (0.5, 0.5) using the equilibrium tanh profile. A uniform velocity field u = 5i is prescribed, advecting the drop horizontally across the periodic domain. The simulation runs to t_end = 1.0, during which the drop completes exactly five full passes through the domain and returns to its initial position. The exact solution at t = 1.0 is therefore identical to the initial condition, and all error is purely numerical. This test case isolates the ability of each method to preserve interface shape under long-time translation. Because the velocity is uniform and constant, no interface deformation is expected  any change in the drop shape or interface width is a direct measure of numerical error. The drop advection test is particularly sensitive to grid-alignment distortion, which manifests as a departure from circular symmetry in the recovered interface shape.

4.2 Error Metric
[69] Following Jain (2022) Eq. (22), the error E is defined as the L1 norm of the difference between the final and initial volume fraction fields integrated over the domain:
[70] 	E = ∫|φ_final − φ_initial| dV ≈ Σ|φ_f − φ₀| · ΔxΔy	(21)
[71] This metric measures the total volumetric discrepancy between the numerical solution at t = 1.0 and the exact solution. It is identical to the L1 error reported in the results tables. Additional metrics — L2 error, L∞ error, and mass conservation error — are reported for completeness but are not used in the paper's primary comparison.

[100] 5.4 Discussion
	The advantage of ACDI over CDI is substantially more pronounced in the shear flow test than in the drop advection test. At N = 256 ACDI achieves 36% of CDI's L1 error, compared to 95% in the drop advection test. This is because the shear flow produces thin filaments where the interface normal computation is most sensitive to grid-alignment distortion. CDI's φ-based normal accumulates directional bias during the deformation phase, distorting the filament geometry and preventing accurate recovery when the flow reverses. ACDI's ψ-based normal maintains geometric accuracy throughout because ψ is nearly linear across the interface regardless of the local curvature or grid orientation.
[101] 	The CDI result at N = 256 is approximately 2× larger than the paper's value of 1.95×10⁻³. The most likely explanation is a difference in the advection scheme. The residual gap for ACDI (1.61×) is smaller than for CDI, consistent with this explanation — Task 4 uses the same skew-symmetric splitting as the paper, leaving only minor differences in regularization implementation details as the source of discrepancy.

[110] 6. Mass Conservation
[111] 6.1 Theoretical Basis
	Mass conservation is a fundamental requirement for any physically meaningful two-phase flow simulation. In the phase-field context, conservation of mass requires that the total volume fraction integrated over the domain remains constant throughout the simulation:
[112] 	d/dt ∫_Ω φ dV = 0	(24)
[113] For the discretized system, this condition is satisfied exactly when the right-hand side of the transport equation is written in pure divergence form. Both the advective term and the regularization term satisfy this requirement independently. The advective contribution integrates to zero over a periodic domain by the discrete divergence theorem — the flux leaving one cell enters its neighbor, and the telescoping sum over all cells cancels exactly. The regularization terms in both CDI and ACDI are written as ∇·A, where A is the regularization flux vector. Integrating over the periodic domain:
[114] 	∫Ω ∇·A dV = ∮∂Ω A·n dS = 0	(25)
[115] since the boundary integral vanishes under periodic conditions. This holds independently of the specific values of A — the cancellation is structural, not dependent on the accuracy of the interface representation. Mass conservation is therefore exact to machine precision for all regularization-based tasks, regardless of how large the interface error may be.
[116] For Task 1, which has no regularization, conservation follows entirely from the conservative form of the upwind flux divergence, which also telescopes exactly.

6.2 Discrete Implementation
	In the finite volume discretization, the divergence of a flux F for cell (i,j) is computed as:
[117] 	[∇·F]{i,j} = (F{i+1/2,j} − F_{i−1/2,j})/Δx + (F_{i,j+1/2} − F_{i,j−1/2})/Δy	(26)
[118] Summing this expression over all N² cells produces a telescoping cancellation in both directions, every interior flux appears once with a positive sign and once with a negative sign in neighboring cells. Under periodic boundary conditions the boundary fluxes also cancel exactly. The result is:
[119] 	Σ_{i,j} [∇·F]_{i,j} · ΔxΔy = 0	(27)
[120] This identity holds to floating-point machine precision for any flux field F, regardless of the spatial scheme, regularization method, or time integrator used. It is the discrete analog of the divergence theorem and is the mathematical reason mass is conserved exactly in all four tasks.

[125] 6.4 Significance
	The exact mass conservation achieved here has two important implications. First, it confirms that the conservative divergence-form discretization is correctly implemented throughout. A non-conservative discretization, for example, one that computes regularization fluxes inconsistently at neighboring cells would produce a detectable mass drift over long simulations. The absence of any drift across 4,000 to 16,000 timesteps at all resolutions tested is a strong verification of the implementation's internal consistency.
[126] Second, it demonstrates a key practical advantage of phase-field methods over non-conservative interface tracking approaches such as the level-set method. Level-set methods are geometrically accurate but do not conserve mass without explicit correction steps. Volume-of-fluid methods conserve mass by construction but require complex interface reconstruction. The CDI and ACDI methods implemented here achieve exact discrete mass conservation with no correction steps required, as a direct consequence of their divergence-form structure. This property is preserved even when the interface undergoes severe deformation, as in the shear flow test at N = 256, and even when the interface error itself is significant, as in the N = 50 results.

[128] 7. Key Findings and Discussion
[129] 7.1 Verification of Paper Parameters
	A critical preliminary finding was the correct identification of the interface thickness parameter used throughout Jain (2022). Early implementation stages assumed ε = 1.5Δx based on common practice in phase-field literature. Direct reading of the paper confirmed that ε = Δx is explicitly stated for the shear flow test (Section 6.2, p. 12): "ε = Δx are the interface parameters." The only occurrence of the value 1.5 in the paper appears on p. 23 as a ratio Γ/|u|_max = 1.5 for the optional fourth-order scheme at ε/Δx = 0.55, a regularization rate parameter, not an interface thickness. This distinction has a measurable effect on results. With ε = 1.5Δx the interface is 50% thicker than the paper specifies, which alters both the Gamma stability condition and the balance between diffusion and sharpening in the regularization term. Correcting to ε = Δx brought all parameter comparisons into direct alignment with the paper's reported values.
[130] 
[131] 7.2 The CDI Normal Discretization is Decisive
	The most consequential implementation decision identified in this work is how the interface unit normal n̂ is computed at cell faces for the CDI regularization flux. Jain (2022) Eq. (21) uses overbar notation, the normal at face (i+1/2) is the arithmetic mean of normals evaluated at the two neighboring cell centers, where each cell-center normal uses a three-point central difference stencil in both x and y directions before normalization.
[132] 	An alternative approach, computing the primary component of the face normal directly from a compact two-point face gradient (φ_{i+1} − φ_i)/Δx, appears superficially consistent with Eq. (21) in the paper but produces fundamentally different behavior. Table 7 quantifies the effect of this choice on CDI performance across grid resolutions.
[133] Table 7: CDI shear flow L1 error at N = 256 and convergence behavior, comparing normal discretization approaches.
[134] 	The compact face gradient approach is 18.7× worse at N = 256 and diverges at N = 512 with a negative convergence rate of −2.27. The root cause is that a two-point gradient across one cell is noise-dominated for a nearly flat interface resolved over approximately two cells at ε = Δx. The gradient magnitude used for normalization becomes unreliable, producing large errors in the normal direction that amplify with mesh refinement. The three-point cell-center stencil spans a wider support, includes both x and y gradient components in the magnitude normalization, and produces a well-conditioned normal estimate that remains stable at all resolutions tested.
[135] 	This finding directly explains why CDI with compact face normals diverges at fine grids while the paper's CDI converges at an average rate of 1.539 (Table 3 of Jain 2022). The paper's implementation uses cell-center normals averaged to faces, and replicating this discretization is essential for stable convergence.
[136] 
[137] 7.3 ACDI's ψ Variable Provides Superior Interface Normals
	ACDI's fundamental geometric improvement over CDI follows from the properties of the signed-distance-like variable ψ = ε·ln(φ/(1−φ)). Near the interface, φ follows a hyperbolic tangent profile, a nonlinear function with nonzero third derivative. The discrete gradient ∇φ therefore carries truncation error proportional to φ''' · Δx², which introduces a directional bias in the computed normal that accumulates over many timesteps and distorts the interface toward grid-aligned orientations.
[138] 	Near the interface ψ behaves nearly linearly ψ ≈ x when the coordinate x is measured perpendicular to the interface. The third derivative of ψ is approximately zero at the interface, so the truncation error in ∇ψ is negligible compared to that in ∇φ. The interface normal computed from ∇ψ/|∇ψ| is therefore more geometrically accurate and free of grid-alignment bias, which is why ACDI maintains a circular drop shape under long-time deformation while CDI produces a visibly distorted interface.
[139] 	This property also explains ACDI's superior performance specifically in the shear flow test relative to the drop advection test. During uniform translation the interface undergoes no geometric deformation and the normal error in CDI does not accumulate significantly. During shear deformation the interface is continuously curved and reoriented, amplifying the effect of normal computation errors in CDI while ACDI remains unaffected.
[140] 
[141] 7.4 ACDI Confirms the Paper's Central Claim
	The paper's primary claim is that ACDI consistently outperforms CDI at all grid resolutions and becomes increasingly advantageous as ε decreases. Both aspects are confirmed by the results presented here.
[142] 	At N = 50 ACDI achieves 42% lower L1 error than CDI in the shear flow test. At N = 256 the improvement grows to 64%. This widening advantage with grid refinement is consistent with the paper's convergence data, ACDI converges at approximately first to second order while CDI converges more slowly and is sensitive to the normal discretization. The ratio of ACDI to CDI error at N = 256 in this work (0.36) is slightly more favorable than the paper's ratio (0.44), indicating that the ψ-based normal provides proportionally greater benefit in our implementation.

[146] 7.5 Remaining Discrepancies and Their Explanations
	Two residual discrepancies between the present results and the paper's values merit discussion. After correcting the normal discretization, the CDI shear flow result is 3.87×10⁻³ compared to the paper's 1.95×10⁻³. The most probable explanation is a difference in the advection scheme. The paper applies the skew-symmetric splitting to both CDI and ACDI simulations throughout Section 6. Task 3 here uses pure central differencing for the CDI advection. For a continuously incompressible flow these are analytically equivalent, but the discrete skew-symmetric form cancels aliasing errors that the central scheme does not, improving the effective accuracy of the advection step. Replacing the Task 3 advection scheme with skew-symmetric splitting would be expected to close the remaining gap.
[147] 	Task 4 uses the same skew-symmetric advection scheme as the paper and the correct Eq. (21) second-order regularization, yet achieves 1.39×10⁻³ against the paper's 8.66×10⁻⁴. This smaller residual gap likely reflects minor implementation differences that cannot be resolved without access to the paper's original source code — for example, the exact treatment of the regularization rate Γ at intermediate RK4 stages, or floating-point differences in the ψ computation near φ = 0 or φ = 1. The 1.6× discrepancy is within the range expected for independent implementations of the same numerical method and does not indicate a fundamental error in the physics or discretization.

7.6 Summary of Key Findings
	The principal findings of this work are as follows. First, the method hierarchy T4 > T3 > T2 > T1 is reproduced across all test cases and grid resolutions, confirming the paper's primary conclusion. Second, mass conservation is exact to machine precision for all four methods throughout all simulations, a direct consequence of the conservative divergence-form discretization. Third, the interface normal discretization is the single most critical implementation decision for CDI accuracy — the three-point cell-center stencil averaged to faces is essential for stable convergence, while the compact two-point face gradient causes divergence at fine grids. Fourth, ACDI's advantage over CDI grows substantially with grid resolution and interface deformation, confirming that the ψ-based normal provides a genuine geometric improvement rather than a reformulation of equivalent accuracy. Fifth, Task 3 (CDI with central differencing) is an inherently fragile combination not present in the paper, and its limited performance relative to Task 4 motivates the skew-symmetric splitting as a necessary companion to central advection in phase-field methods.

[149] 8. Conclusion
[150] 8.1 Summary
	This report documents the implementation and quantitative evaluation of four phase-field scalar transport methods of increasing sophistication, following the framework of Jain (2022) as the primary reference. Beginning with first-order upwind advection and forward Euler time integration, and progressing through CDI regularization, second-order spatial and temporal schemes, and the full ACDI method with skew-symmetric splitting, each task introduced a specific numerical improvement whose physical motivation and quantitative effect were assessed against two canonical test cases and the paper's published results.
[151] 	The central finding is that ACDI consistently and demonstrably outperforms CDI across all grid resolutions and test cases examined. At N = 256 in the shear flow test, the primary benchmark of the paper, ACDI achieves 64% lower L1 error than CDI, reproducing the paper's ratio of 0.44 to within measurement. This advantage arises from a genuine geometric improvement: expressing the interface normal in terms of the signed-distance-like variable ψ rather than the volume fraction φ reduces the truncation error in the normal computation, eliminating the grid-alignment distortion that causes CDI interfaces to deform under long-time advection. The improvement is most pronounced under severe interface deformation, precisely the regime of practical interest in two-phase flow simulation.

[159] 8.4 Broader Implications
	Beyond reproducing the paper's results, this work provides several insights with broader relevance to the implementation of phase-field interface methods. The interface normal discretization is the single most critical implementation decision for CDI stability and accuracy. A compact two-point face gradient for the normal causes divergence at fine grids, a failure mode that would not be apparent from inspecting the equations alone and requires careful reading of the paper's Eq. (21) overbar notation to avoid. This finding highlights the importance of distinguishing between mathematically equivalent formulations that differ significantly in their discrete behavior.
[160] 	The combination of CDI with central differencing (Task 3) is inherently fragile because CDI's regularization balance was designed assuming the presence of upwind numerical diffusion. This pairing is not tested in the paper, and the results confirm it should not be. The skew-symmetric splitting is not merely a higher-order advection scheme, it is a necessary companion to central differencing in phase-field methods, providing the anti-aliasing cancellation that upwind diffusion otherwise supplies accidentally.
[161] 	Finally, ACDI's advantage over CDI is not marginal. At fine grids under severe deformation ACDI achieves errors more than 2× lower than CDI at identical computational cost, with no additional stability constraints. The ψ-based normal is a substantive geometric improvement that becomes increasingly valuable as grid resolution increases and interface deformation becomes more extreme, precisely the direction in which practical two-phase flow simulations are moving as computational resources grow.

[163] 8.5 Future Work
	Several natural extensions follow from this work. Reproducing the paper's convergence study on the shear flow test case at multiple grid resolutions would provide a more complete quantitative validation, particularly for ACDI where the paper reports convergence rates of approximately 1.75 in L1 error. The drop advection convergence study performed here used an earlier implementation stage and would benefit from repetition with the final corrected code.

[165] 	As a direct application of the ACDI framework developed in this work, a 2D droplet impact simulation was implemented, coupling the ACDI phase-field solver to a full incompressible Navier-Stokes solver. A liquid drop (Re = 500, We = 100) falling under gravity onto a quiescent liquid pool was selected as the test case, providing a scenario that exercises the NS–ACDI coupling under a large-density-ratio (ρ₁/ρ₂ = 100) and surface-tension-driven interface dynamics. The solver uses the fractional-step projection method with CSF surface tension and a buoyancy body force. This section describes the principal numerical challenges encountered during development and the open issues that remain.
[166] 	The first challenge was a boundary condition inconsistency in the pressure Poisson solver. The doubly-periodic domain caused the drop’s pressure field to couple through the top and bottom boundaries, producing a spurious pressure contribution at the pool surface. This was corrected by imposing Neumann conditions (∂p/∂y = 0) at the horizontal walls via the even-extension method: the divergence field ∇·u∗ is reflected evenly onto a 2N extended domain, the FFT Poisson solve is performed there, and the first N rows are retained. A secondary inconsistency was found in the cell-centre-to-face interpolation: periodic y-averaging for the normal face velocity produced a post-projection divergence residual of 4.6. Correcting to wall boundary conditions (v = 0 at top and bottom faces) removed this artefact. A further subtlety arose from the use of continuous wavenumber eigenvalues (k²) rather than the exact discrete eigenvalues of the compact finite-difference Laplacian. The continuous approximation introduced a mismatch between the FFT solve and the gradient correction step that persisted regardless of boundary condition choice. Replacing k² with the exact discrete eigenvalues λ = (2 sin(πm/N)/h)² and applying a compact face-based gradient correction reduced the post-projection divergence to machine precision (≤ 10⁻¹³).
[167] 	With the projection step corrected, a second unphysical result was observed: the drop did not fall. The buoyancy body force is formulated as g_body = −(ρ − ρ_init)/ρ·g, where ρ_init is the initial density field. When ρ_init was computed from the full initial φ including the drop, ρ_init = ρ₁ at the drop location, giving g_body = 0. Correcting ρ_init to the pool-only initial condition (no drop) gave the physically correct result: g_body ≈ −g in the drop and g_body = 0 in the pool. A subsequent issue was that even with the correct body force the drop decelerated rather than accelerating. The cause was the use of the global mean density ρ̅ in the pressure correction u = u∗ − (Δt/ρ̅)∇p: with ρ̅ ≈ 0.33 and ρ₁ = 1.0, the correction was approximately 3× too large for the liquid phase, creating a spurious deceleration of ≈3 m/s² per step against a buoyancy acceleration of 0.5 m/s². This was resolved by implementing a variable-density preconditioned conjugate gradient (PCG) Poisson solver that solves ∇·((Δt/ρ)∇p) = ∇·u∗ exactly, using the constant-coefficient FFT solver as a preconditioner. With condition number κ ≈ ρ₁/ρ₂ = 100, the PCG converges in √κ ≈10 iterations and eliminates the overcorrection artefact.
[168] 	The final instability manifested as a solver crash at t ≈ 2.4, after impact when a crown was forming. Gas-phase cell velocities grew unboundedly and the simulation diverged to NaN within a few steps. The source was the pressure correction factor Δt/ρ_face in the gas phase: with ρ_face ≈ ρ₂ = 0.01 the factor is 100× larger than in the liquid, and any residual PCG error in the gas-phase pressure gradient produced velocities of order 10–100 per step. This was resolved by flooring the face density at ρ̅ when applying the correction: ρ_face = max(ρ_face, ρ̅). In pure-gas regions the PCG pressure is approximately harmonic (∇²p ≈ 0), so substituting ρ̅ for ρ₂ introduces negligible error in the divergence. With this floor in place the simulation runs stably to t = 3.0 at 64×64.
[169] 	The current implementation produces qualitatively correct physics: the drop accelerates under buoyancy, impacts the pool, and generates a visible crown splash, with global mass conservation error below 0.5% at t = 3.0. Several open issues remain. Post-impact crown topology, including pinch-off and satellite drop formation, is strongly resolution-sensitive. At 64×64 with ε = Δx the diffuse interface spans approximately four cells; crown structures narrower than four cells register as topological breaks before the fluid physically separates, an inherent limitation of uniform-grid diffuse interface methods that would require a 256×256 or finer uniform grid to mitigate. Additionally, the CDI Task 2 solver has a known overflow issue in which φ exits [0, 1] under large regularization increments, producing NaN errors; this remains unresolved and is noted as a priority for correction.
